# PROMPTS — CredLens AI Spend Audit

This document details the LLM prompt design, structure, and engineering tradeoffs made for the AI Spend Summary feature of **CredLens**.

---

## 📝 Active Summary Generation Prompt

Our Next.js API endpoint `/api/summary/route.ts` sends the following prompt to the Google Gemini API (`gemini-2.5-flash`):

```text
You are a startup CFO and AI engineering operations expert advising a founder of a ${teamSize}-person team who primarily uses their stack for "${useCase}".
Here is their audited monthly AI spend data:
- Total Current Monthly Spend: $${totalCurrentSpend}
- Potential Monthly Savings Identified: $${totalMonthlySavings}
- Potential Annual Savings: $${totalAnnualSavings}

Here are the specific recommendations generated by our rules engine:
${recommendationsText}

Task: Write a concise, professional, and actionable executive summary paragraph of about 80-120 words.
Focus on explaining where their biggest savings leverage is (e.g. redundant IDE licenses, seat counts, API prompt caching, or credit swapping) and why consolidating makes sense. Make the advice sound direct, analytical, and tailored to their team size. Avoid generic fluff. Do not mention "as an AI" or "based on the rules". Start directly with the core analysis.
```

---

## 🔍 Why We Wrote the Prompt This Way

1. **Explicit Role Assumption**: We set the persona to a *"startup CFO and AI engineering operations expert"*. This ensures the output tone is financial, realistic, and operationally literate rather than a generic summary.
2. **Contextual Variables**: We pass `teamSize` and `useCase` to anchor the narrative around their specific operational realities (e.g. a small coding team gets advice about IDE license waste, whereas a mixed operational team gets advice about chat interface redundancies).
3. **Structured Inputs**: Formatting the rule output into a clean, bulleted list (`recommendationsText`) allows the model to summarize the core findings efficiently without getting confused about calculations.
4. **Length and Structure Constraints**: Restricting the output to *80-120 words* and asking it to start directly with the core analysis prevents generic greeting fluff (e.g. "Here is your report...") and guarantees the summary fits neatly inside our visual UI glass-panels.

---

## ❌ What We Tried That Didn't Work

### 1. Asking the LLM to Calculate the Math
- **Approach**: In early tests, we passed the raw list of tools and costs to the model and asked it to calculate the optimal plans and overall savings amounts itself.
- **Why it failed**: Large Language Models are notoriously prone to arithmetic hallucinations. It frequently failed on minimum seat calculations (e.g., assuming Claude Team Standard is always $25/user without checking the 5-seat minimum), leading to mathematically incorrect recommendations.
- **Resolution**: We restricted calculations entirely to a deterministic TypeScript rule engine (`analyzer.ts`) and utilized the LLM strictly to synthesize the results. Knowing when *not* to use AI is a core product design principle.

### 2. Letting the LLM output Markdown Formatting
- **Approach**: Asking the LLM to write a summarized table or use list elements.
- **Why it failed**: The output layout was inconsistent, sometimes breaking the responsiveness of our results dashboard card overlays or overflowing on mobile screens.
- **Resolution**: We constrained the model to output a single, solid paragraph and structured the visual layout of individual cards directly inside React.
